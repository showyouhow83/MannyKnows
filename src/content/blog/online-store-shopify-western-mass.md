"Just put it on Shopify." You've heard that. And it's true — you can spin up a Shopify store in an afternoon. What nobody tells you is that a store you set up in an afternoon usually sells about as much as it took to build: nothing. The gap between "I have an online store" and "my online store sells" is real work. Here's what it actually takes.

## Setting up a store is easy. Selling is the job.

Shopify makes the plumbing easy — that's exactly why we use it. The hard part was never the software. It's everything that turns a page of products into a shop people actually buy from. Skip that part and you've built a nicely designed dead end.

## Your products have to earn the click

This is where most stores lose. Every product needs:

- **Real photos** — clean, well-lit, from a few angles, showing scale. A phone photo on a messy counter doesn't sell; it raises doubt.
- **Copy that answers questions** — size, materials, what's included, why it's good. Shoppers can't pick it up and turn it over, so the words have to do that for them.
- **Variants set up right** — sizes, colors, bundles — so buying is one obvious click, not a puzzle.

Multiply that by your whole catalog and you see why "set up a store" is the small part.

## Payments, shipping, and taxes — the unglamorous half

None of this is optional, and getting it wrong quietly kills sales:

- **Payments and checkout** configured so people can actually pay, on their phone, without friction.
- **Shipping** — real rates, real boxes, real zones. Surprise shipping costs at checkout are the top reason carts get abandoned.
- **Taxes** — set up correctly for where you sell, so you're not untangling it at tax time.

Restaurants and makers especially trip here: selling a physical product online is a different machine than ringing someone up at the counter.

## Emails do the selling you're not there for

Most people don't buy on the first visit. Email automations bring them back — automatically:

- a **welcome series** for new subscribers,
- an **abandoned-cart** nudge for the ones who almost bought,
- **receipts and follow-ups** that feel personal, not robotic.

Set up once, these run in the background and quietly recover sales you'd otherwise lose.

## An AI shopping assistant closes the gap

In a real shop, someone helps: "What goes with this?" "Do you have it in blue?" "Which one for a gift?" Online, that person is usually missing — and the shopper just leaves. An **AI shopping assistant** puts that help back: it answers product questions, points people to the right item, and helps them decide 24/7, in your customer's language. It's the difference between a shelf and a salesperson.
