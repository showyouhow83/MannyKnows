Two businesses show up side by side in Google. Same distance, same service. One has 12 reviews, the other has 140. Which one gets the call? You already know. Reviews are one of the strongest signals for where you land in local search **and** the first thing a customer reads before they choose. For a clinic or a law firm, that's often the whole ballgame — the vast majority of people check reviews before they'll book a provider or a lawyer.

Good news: getting more of them isn't about tricks. It's a system. Here it is.

## Just ask — every time, right away

The single biggest reason businesses don't have reviews is that they don't ask. Most happy customers are glad to leave one; they just forget the moment they walk out. So ask **every** satisfied customer, and ask when the good feeling is fresh — right after the job's done, the case closed, the appointment finished.

## Make it a ten-second task

"Look us up on Google and leave a review" is too much work, so it doesn't happen. Instead, hand them a **direct link** that opens straight to the review box — as a text, a QR code on the counter or invoice, or a line in your follow-up email. The easier you make it, the more you get. This one change usually does more than anything else.

## Build it into how you already work

Don't rely on remembering. Wire the ask into your routine: the text that confirms a job is complete, the email receipt, the discharge or closing paperwork. When the request goes out automatically at the right moment, reviews stop being a thing you chase and become a thing that just happens.

## Reply to every one — good and bad

Replying matters more than owners think. For good reviews, a quick, specific thank-you shows you're paying attention. For bad ones, a calm, professional reply is **for the next customer**, not the angry one — because everyone reading sees how you handle a problem. A thoughtful response to a one-star review earns more trust than the review costs you.

## The rules you can't break

- **Never buy fake reviews.** Google detects them, removes them, and can penalize you. It's not worth it.
- **Never offer a discount or gift for a review.** That violates Google's policy (and the FTC's) and can get your reviews wiped. Ask for honest feedback, not paid praise.
- **Don't ask them all at once.** Fifty reviews in one day looks fake to Google. A steady trickle beats a spike — recency counts anyway.

## A note for medical and legal

You've got extra rules — HIPAA for healthcare, bar advertising rules for attorneys. You can still absolutely ask for reviews; just keep your **replies** generic and never confirm someone was a patient or client or discuss any detail of their care or case. "Thank you for the kind words" is always safe. When in doubt, say less.
